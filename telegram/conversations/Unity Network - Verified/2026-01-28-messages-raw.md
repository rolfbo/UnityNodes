Don Standard, [Jan 28, 2026 at 12:24]
Building on the discussion above, here’s how I’m currently thinking about it. 

Setting an artificially low ULO profit share on the marketplace won’t prevent undercutting. It will simply push competition off-platform into Telegram groups and DMs. 

That still results in marketeers losing ULOs they onboarded, while new users are flooded with offers and exposed to bad actors. It also creates a real risk of scams, which reflects badly on Unity as a platform. 

Another unintended consequence is that, if organic leasing becomes harder for some UNOs, it may incentivise consolidation of devices rather than growth of a distributed operator base — which runs counter to the goal of a broad, decentralised edge network. 

A cleaner solution would be for Unity to apply a consistent, platform-defined licence split wherever licences are offered. If no one can offer preferential terms elsewhere, there’s no incentive for DM outreach, licence switching, or spam.
People who put real work into marketing Unity would still have a fair advantage by being able to distribute their own lease codes. Those who don’t wish to, or are unable to market, would still have their licences fairly allocated to new ULOs who join without a code. 

This isn’t about restricting competition — it’s about clear, transparent rules applied equally, protecting users, and keeping activity on the platform rather than in private messages.

CloverNodes, [Jan 28, 2026 at 18:55]
Your proposal is great and I have to give you credit for taking the time, it solves a lot of issues but it introduces some fundamental changes that need to be addressed:

**1) Automatic assignment breaks the incentive loop:** The proposal removes the marketplace and does automatic assignment on install (round-robin / load-balanced / performance weighted)
 The issue is simple, if I bring a user to Unity and they get assigned to someone else why would I market? The proposal tries to solve this with referrals later but that still creates a “marketing dead zone” in the meantime.

**2) It removes ULO choice and community trust:** This proposal makes onboarding “simpler” because users no longer choose operators. But many ULOs want to pick an operator because:
A) They trust them
B) They want support
C) They’re part of that operator’s community

Random assignment makes UNOs interchangeable. That kills relationships, loyalty and the most important part it kills retention.

**3) “Protocol-defined” becomes political fast:** Akira says it would be neutral and protocol-defined. But once you introduce:
- performance weighted assignment
-uptime scoring
-quality metrics
you’ve created the next battleground. Now everyone fights over the algorithm instead of the marketplace.

**4) Referral systems create a new type of abuse:** He does include safeguards like:
-time-locked rewards
-one-time attribution
-caps / decay
-device fingerprinting + wallet -uniqueness + rate limits

That’s good, but in practice referral systems attract:
-fake installs
-churn loops
-phone farms
-referral laundering
So you may remove split wars, but you introduce referral warfare.

**5) The timing is backwards:** In the doc, referrals really “pay” when the network reaches capacity and licenses are scarce, like via airdrops. But incentives are needed most RIGHT NOW, while adoption is still being built. You don’t remove the main incentive today and promise rewards later.
Akira’s proposal is thoughtful and I agree with the problem it’s trying to solve, race-to-the-bottom splits are not healthy. But removing the marketplace entirely breaks the incentive loop for UNOs to market, it also removes ULO choice and it shifts conflict from splits to an algorithm.

**The better fix is simple:** keep the marketplace, set a minimum standard split, keep private deals free market and add referrals as a bonus layer on the ULO side to drive adoption.  That keeps builders incentivized today while still eliminating the worst marketplace behavior.

Referral systems sound great on paper but they always attract people trying to game it. Fake installs are when someone downloads the app just to trigger a referral reward then never use it.

Churn loops happen when people activate for the minimum time.. cash out whatever reward then drop off and repeat.

Phone farms someone runs 20 to 200 phones to simulate “new users” and farms referrals which is hard to combat 

Referral laundering are when groups trade referral codes and recycle signups to make it look like real growth, even though it’s just the same circle of people. So you might remove split wars in the marketplace but you replace it with a different battle who’s is people competing to exploit referrals instead of competing to provide better value.

The proposal does mention time-locks, one-time attribution and Sybil resistance which helps but it’s still a real risk in any referral-based system which in my opinion needs to be thought of very well before being introduced
