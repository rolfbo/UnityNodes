Nico | NodeX, [Jan 5, 2026 at 13:19]
On gamification..

“We are not creating a game.”
They’re adding gamification elements, not turning Unity into a game.
It wasn’t a marketing choice — it came from weeks of internal debate.
The “Scout & Runner” poster/characters are the “front cover” of the first third-party task on Unity’s composable network.

Why use gamification?
To force structure, clarity, and correctness in a task that needs real telecom precision.

The goal is guided behavior:
do the task “the right way”
without vulnerabilities/exploits
without misunderstandings

and with clear “what success looks like” It’s meant to make users feel safe and confident:
no anxiety
no second guessing
no “is this working?” uncertainty

After deciding on gamification, Omri says he brought in a UX gamification expert (designed complex gamified systems for big consumer products, enterprise platforms, and a large educational system) to build the experience properly.

OK, I wanted to get a little more clarity on the Scout and Runner comms event so used a little speech to text and then queried it a little...
Scout — definition and responsibilities

A Scout is a discovery-and-verification role that operates at the edge of the Unity network.

From the meeting:

What actions a Scout performs:
Operates with a real device, real SIM, and real carrier connectivity.
Uncovers telecom information that exists locally or can be reached remotely.
Targets information that is hard to find and very hard to automate.

Actively verifies discovered information by:
Activating a SIM.
Confirming voice, SMS, and data functionality on the target network.

What signals it generates
Produces verified telecom facts (reachability, network behavior, identity of routes or interconnect characteristics as applicable to the task).
Once verified, the data becomes trusted input for the network.

How Unity uses those signals
Verified Scout data is ingested into Unity as trusted groundwork.
This data enables downstream execution tasks (Runner tasks).
Scout outputs form the first step in a closed operational loop.

Requirements, constraints, examples
Scouts must use real SIMs on real carrier networks.
No improvisation: verification must be exact.
All credits and top-ups required (SIM, calls, SMS, data) are paid by the issuer, not the operator.
If the information is correct and verified, the Scout earns.
Conceptual framing used by the speaker: “Discover” is the Scout phase.

Runner — definition and responsibilities
A Runner is an execution role that acts on trusted information produced by Scouts.

From the meeting:
What actions a Runner performs
Operates with an active SIM on the concerned network.
Executes pre-defined, non-improvised actions, such as:
Making calls.
Sending messages.
Using data in a specified way.
Performs tasks exactly as configured by the issuer.

How it differs from Scout
Scout discovers and verifies information.
Runner executes deterministic actions using that verified information.
Scout work establishes trust; Runner work exploits that trust to perform measurable telecom tests.

What signals it generates
Produces execution results from live telecom activity:
Call behavior.
Messaging behavior.
Data usage outcomes.
Each completed Runner task emits a measurable result and earns a reward.

Requirements, constraints, examples
Accuracy is emphasized repeatedly: “nothing is improvised and accuracy matters.”
Runner tasks depend on Scout outputs and form a handshake / loop with them.
Conceptual framing used by the speaker: “Execute” is the Runner phase.

UNO Operator tasks and expectations
From the meeting, UNO operators are expected to act as hands-on participants, not passive node holders.
Onboarding steps
Register via Unity-provided forms for specific release groups (closed alpha → beta → public).
Meet defined criteria for participation (varies by release group).
Operate real devices with real SIMs on real carrier networks.
Daily or periodic responsibilities
Run Scout and/or Runner tasks as assigned.
Perform real telecom actions (verification or execution) exactly as specified.
Actively test usability and task flow during early release phases.
Interaction with Scout and Runner task families

Scouts: discover and verify hard-to-automate telecom information.
Runners: execute defined telecom actions using Scout-verified data.

Operators may participate in one or both roles depending on eligibility and task design.

What success looks like
Accurate, repeatable execution at scale.
A system that works not just for telecom experts, but can scale from hundreds to millions of participants.
Data that is trusted, usable, and valuable to issuing telecom partners.
